The Computer Misuse Act (1990)

What is the Computer Misuse Act?

The Computer Misuse Act (CMA) is a UK law that was introduced in 1990 to address the issue of Cybercrime. It is designed to protect computer users and systems from unauthorised access, hacking and other forms of Cybercrime. The law itself has three main sections, which are outlined below.

  • Section 1: Unauthorised access to computer material
    • This provision of the law makes it illegal to gain access to any computer systems or data on those systems without proper permission and authorisation. It also covers any methods that would be used to gain access - such as hacking, social engineering and use of malware.
    • Section 2: Unauthorized access with intent to commit or facilitate a crime
    • This provision of the law makes it illegal to gain access to any computer systems or data on those systems with the intent to commit or facilitate a crime. This is a more serious offence than the first provision, as it is a criminal act in itself. This could include stealing data, distributing viruses or malware, or using the system to commit fraud.
  • Section 3: Unauthorized modification of computer material
    • This section makes it an offence to modify computer material without proper authorisation. This includes deleting, corrupting or altering data or programs.

Penalties for Breaking this Law

The penalties for breaking this law depend on the severity of the crime. For less serious offences the penalty could be a fine of up to £5000 or two years in prison. For more serious offences , such as those involing significant financial loss or damage, the penalty could be up to ten years in prison!

Notable Case

Lewys Martin - May 13th, 2013

In 2013, Lewys Martin was convicted for various breaches of the Computer Misuse Act and received the following sentences:

  • 5 offences of unauthorised modification of computer material - 2 years in prison.
    • DDOS and SQL injection attack on Oxford University.
    • Disruption to the University website, services and business.
    • DOS attack which disrupted the Cambridge University's Science Week programme and online event booking system.
    • DOS attack on the Kent Police website, causing the server to be shut down for 30 minutes.
    • A handwritten list of additional 'possible targets' recovered from his grandparent's address by police, with targets including SOCA, the BBC, the Army, Oxford Cambridge Uni, Kent Met Police, Channel 5 TV, CIA, NSA, Sony, HMBC and major news organisations.
  • 1 offence of securing unauthorised access with intent to commit crime - 12 months in prison.
    • He stole the passwords, bank account details, and credit card numbers from one of his clients when he worked as a self-employed computer repairman. He used them to buy a pizza from Domino's, and denied having done so when challenged by the person he stole them from. These details were found on a mobile phone that he had on his person, and additional details on other people were found in a file called 'Bank Hack' on his computer when it was seized by police.
  • 1 offence of securing unauthorised access to computer material - 6 months in prison.
    • He made an anonymous telephone call to one of his victims, telling them that their personal details were all available online, and changed the password to one of their online bank accounts to prove it.

    • He was also convicted of two counts of making, supplying or obtaining articles for use in breaching section 3 of the CMA - Installing software on his computer (Jaindos) for the purpose of orchestrating DoS attacks, as well as software for camoflaguing your IP address (Cyber Ghost). For this he received an additional 4 months in prison.

    That's a total of 3 years and 10 months in prison, which was still quite lenient according to the case transcript!
